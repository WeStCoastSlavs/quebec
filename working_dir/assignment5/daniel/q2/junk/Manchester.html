Manchester is the city at the centre of Greater Manchester, one of the largest built-up areas in England outside London. The area of the official district called the 'city of Manchester' is quite small, because many parts of urban Manchester lie in the surrounding boroughs which are not counted in the statistics. Manchester is the 2nd city of Northern England, after Liverpool which is larger , and the North West, the part of England it is in.

[change] Geography

Manchester is located 160 miles (257 km) north-west of England's capital city, London. The city has many canals and rivers which played a vital role in its earlier development. The largest open space in the city, at around 618 acres (3 km²), is Heaton Park which is a place attracts many visitors every year because it has many animals and the beauty of the countryside can be seen. Much of Manchester is built-up (or 'urbanised') and as a result, much of the rain does not clear quickly. The city is made up of both urban areas and 'agricultural areas'. Much of Manchester's rain comes over from the Penines, which are a hilly range to the south and east of the city. To the south of the centre is Manchester Airport, which is the third largest airport in the United Kingdom.

[change] History

The Peterloo Massacre of 1819 saw fifteen-twenty deaths and several hundred injured.

In the late 1700s, Manchester was only a small village with a relatively low population. This changed upon the start of the Industrial Revolution. During this period, there were revolts about the living conditions for workers, the most notable being the Peterloo Massacre which ended up with 15-20 people being killed, and hundreds wounded.[1] In 1780, Richard Arkwright built the first cotton mill in the area.[2]

Later in the 1800s, Manchester became the world's most important cotton town, and also the first 'industrialised' city.[3] Only a small amount of cloth is still made in the city and the trade has mainly stopped; leaving a large number of old buildings from that time including the Town Hall, Free Trade Hall and Central Library and leaving a lot of others vacant.

The Manchester Ship Canal was created by canalisation of the Rivers Irwell and Mersey for 36 miles (58 km) from Salford to the Mersey estuary. This enabled ocean going ships to sail right into the Port of Manchester.

The county of Greater Manchester was made in 1974 when the county was mainly formed by cities and towns in southern Lancashire and northern Cheshire (the two main areas which make up Greater Manchester), and some mainly rural districts of Derbyshire and Yorkshire.

In 1996, the Irish Republican Army placed a bomb in a truck, located in the centre of the city. Nobody died, but it caused an extensive amount of damage. [4]

[change] Culture

A revival of the city's importance began in the 1980s. This was partly led by a vibrant music scene whose spirit was labelled 'Madchester', which was led by bands such as The Happy Mondays and Joy Division and also partly fuelled by the large number of students attending the number of universities and further education colleges in the city. Manchester has the highest population of students in the United Kingdom in term time.

The city contains the UK's largest university which is the University of Manchester and has over 30,000 students.[5] It also has the fourth largest university which is the Manchester Metropolitan University.[5]

The city has two premier league football teams, Manchester City and Manchester United. Football plays a big role in the city's culture and many of the population support or show interest in the sport.[6] Some argue that a few teams are not in Manchester, but this is not true.[7] Manchester United are are world-famous and have supporters all over the nationally as well as internationally, many of them dubbed 'glory supporters'.
